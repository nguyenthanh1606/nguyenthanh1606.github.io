Wine laws Spain

Spain wine has a long history from about 4000 to 3000 BC, and is currently the third largest wine producer in the world after Italy and France.

 

Spain created the system of wine laws (Denominacion de Origen - designation of origin - abbreviated DO) in 1932 and then amended in 1970. The hierarchy of the system has many similarities. with the (AOC) system of France, Portugal (DOC) and Italy (DOC). As of 2009, 79 regions across Spain were ranked in this ranking. There are two areas with the highest quality: Rioja and Priorat.

Each DO has a Consejo Regulador, a governing body that controls and enforces regulations and standards related to wine cultivation and production. These rules govern everything from the allowed grape varieties, the maximum harvestable yield, the minimum brewing period and the information that should appear on the wine label. Wineries that want to sell their wine under DO or DOC conditions must send the product to the Consejo Regulador laboratory for testing and evaluation. Alcohol certified DO / DOC will be stamped the area of ​​Consejo Regulador on the label.

After Spain entered the European Union, the Spanish wine law was deemed suitable for other European systems so there was no significant change. The ranks of Spanish wines from low to high are as follows:

Tier 1: Vino de Mesa (VdM) - These are wines that are equivalent to most wines of the country and are made from unclassified vineyards or grapes that have been blended, requiring assurance of sanitary conditions and food safety.

Level 2: Vinos de la Tierra (VdlT) - this level is similar to France's vin de pays rank, geographic information and appears on labels with wide geographic indications such as Andalucia, Castilla La Mancha and Levante .

Level 3: Vinos de Calidad con Indicación Geográfica (VCIG) - Introduced in 2003, this level is similar to France's Vin De'limitee 'de Qualite' Supe'rieure (VDQS) and is considered to be in the bottle of wine. appraisal time to work towards DO. After holding VCIG rankings for 5 consecutive years, the region can apply for an upgrade license to DO.

Level 4: Denominación de Origen (Denominació d'Origen in Catalan-DO) - This level is for good quality wine areas managed by Consejo Regulador ,. In 2005, nearly two-thirds of all vineyards in Spain were in the DO area.

Level 5: Denominación de Origen Calificada (DOCa / DOQ - Denominació d'Origen Qualificada in Catalan) - This choice is similar to Italy's DOCG rank where the region has a consistent and more difficult quality track record. DO. Rioja was the first region to be named in 1991 and followed by Priorat in 2003. In 2008, Ribera del Duero was accepted for DOCa classification.

Level 6: Vino de Pago - also known as Denominación de Pago (DO de Pago) for independent manufacturers with an international reputation. As of 2013, there were 15 manufacturers in Spain that reached this level of this situation.
